# Shuttle Platform ICP - University Programs

University-specific variation of the Shuttle Platform ICP. For shared elements (pain points, core capabilities), reference the [Base Shuttle ICP](./base.md).

**Use this variation when:**
- Target is a university, college, or educational institution
- Program is campus-based student/faculty transportation
- Decision makers are in campus services, transportation, or facilities

### Student Population

### Program Ownership
- **Department:** Transportation Services, Campus Services, Parking & Transportation, or Facilities
- **Staff:** Transportation Director, Shuttle Coordinators, Dispatch, Drivers
- **Reporting Line:** Typically to VP of Campus Operations or Facilities

### Geographic Footprint
- Campus-based routes (main campus and satellite locations)
- Off-campus housing routes
- Parking lot shuttles (remote lots to campus)
- Inter-campus routes (for multi-campus systems)

## Buying Process

### Buying Committee

**Primary Decision Maker:**
- Director of Transportation Services
- Director of Parking & Transportation
- Associate VP of Campus Services

**Economic Buyer:**
- CFO or VP of Business/Finance
- VP of Campus Operations
- Budget authority typically at VP or C-level

**Champion (Day-to-Day User):**
- Transportation Operations Manager
- Shuttle Program Coordinator
- Dispatch Manager

**Key Influencers:**
- **Student Government:** Strong voice on student experience and fees
- **Campus Safety/Police:** Security and late-night service concerns
- **Sustainability Office:** Environmental goals and reporting
- **IT Department:** Technical requirements, SSO integration, data security
- **Facilities/Campus Planning:** Route planning and infrastructure
- **Accessibility Services:** ADA compliance and special needs

**Unique Stakeholder:** Student feedback heavily influences decisions. Student government often has formal input or veto power on major changes.

### Typical Buying Triggers

**Operational Triggers:**
- Current shuttle software contract expiring
- Student complaints about lack of real-time tracking
- Overcrowding and safety concerns
- Service disruptions causing student dissatisfaction
- Difficulty managing route changes (construction, events)

**Strategic Triggers:**
- New student housing construction (off-campus routes needed)
- Campus expansion (satellite locations, new buildings)
- RFP for transportation services (full rebid of program)
- Parking reduction initiatives
- Sustainability goals
- Safety initiatives (improve late-night transportation)

**Financial Triggers:**
- Student fee referendum (need to justify program value)
- Budget cuts requiring efficiency improvements
- Grant funding for sustainable transportation

**External Triggers:**
- Peer institutions adopting modern tracking systems
- Student expectations from consumer apps (Uber-like experience)
- ADA compliance requirements
- Campus security incidents

### Decision Criteria (Ranked)

1. **Student Experience & Satisfaction**
   - Real-time tracking (students demand to know "where's my bus")
   - Easy-to-use mobile app (iOS and Android)
   - Accurate ETAs and route information

2. **Capacity Management & Safety**
   - Prevent overcrowding (safety and liability)
   - Seat availability visibility
   - Late-night service tracking (safety critical)

3. **Multi-Stakeholder Reporting**
   - Ridership data (justify program to administration)
   - Cost per ride and utilization
   - Sustainability metrics (carbon footprint, car trips avoided)

4. **Campus Integration**
   - SSO integration (campus credentials)
   - Student directory integration
   - Campus calendar integration

5. **Ease of Route Management**
   - Quick updates for construction detours
   - Seasonal schedules (adjust for breaks, finals, summer)
   - Special event routing (game days, orientation)

6. **Implementation Timeline**
   - Must launch before fall semester start
   - Pilot during low-volume periods (summer, winter break)

7. **Total Cost of Ownership**
   - Affordable for limited budgets
   - No hidden fees or per-rider charges
   - Scalable pricing

8. **Vendor Stability & Support**
   - Long-term viability
   - Higher education references
   - Dedicated support (not self-service)

### Sales Cycle Characteristics

**Cycle Length:** 4-6 months (often longer due to committee process)

**Typical Timeline:**
- Month 1-2: RFP release and vendor discovery
- Month 3: Proposals submitted and reviewed
- Month 4: Demos and vendor presentations
- Month 5: Reference checks and committee decision
- Month 6: Contract negotiation and approval
- Implementation: 4-8 weeks before semester start

**Best Times to Engage:**
- January-March: Budget planning for next fiscal year
- April-June: Contract renewals and new vendor evaluations
- July-September: Implementation for fall semester
- **Avoid:** November-December (holiday break, end of semester chaos)

### Budget Authority & Deal Size

**Budget Range:**
- Small programs (5-10 vehicles): $10K-$25K annually
- Medium programs (10-25 vehicles): $25K-$60K annually
- Large programs (25+ vehicles): $60K-$150K+ annually

**Funding Sources:**
- Student transportation fees (most common)
- Parking revenue
- General institutional budget
- Sustainability or green initiative grants
- State funding (for public institutions)

**Approval Process:**
- Transportation Director or VP has spending authority up to $25K-$50K
- Above that threshold requires CFO or President approval
- Very large contracts (>$100K) may require Board of Trustees

## University-Specific Pain Points

### 1. Student Dissatisfaction ("Where's My Bus?")

- **Pain:** Students complain constantly about not knowing when shuttles arrive
- **Impact:** Student government pressure, social media complaints, low program perception
- **Language:** "Our students are used to tracking Uber drivers. They expect the same from campus shuttles."

### 2. Overcrowding & Safety Concerns

- **Pain:** Buses fill up, leaving students stranded or creating unsafe crowding
- **Impact:** Safety incidents, liability concerns, campus police involvement, Title IX implications
- **Language:** "We've had buses leave students behind because they were over capacity. It's a safety issue, especially at night."

### 3. Inability to Justify Program Value

- **Pain:** Administration questions shuttle program ROI, especially when budgets are tight
- **Impact:** Budget cuts, service reductions, increased student fees
- **Language:** "When the CFO asks 'Why do we spend $2M on shuttles?', we need data to prove the value."

### 4. Construction & Event Disruptions

- **Pain:** Constant construction on campus requires route changes, hard to communicate
- **Impact:** Student confusion, missed classes, complaints
- **Language:** "We have construction detours every semester. It takes hours to update our website, apps, and signage."

### 5. Late-Night Safety Service Challenges

- **Pain:** Students need late-night rides for safety, but tracking is critical
- **Impact:** Safety concerns, Title IX compliance issues, liability
- **Language:** "Our late-night shuttle is critical for student safety, but we have no way to track if students made it home."

## GTM Motion

### Primary Approach

**Sales-Led with RFP Response:**
- Most universities issue formal RFPs for shuttle software
- Sales process is committee-driven and slow
- Requires detailed proposal and reference customers
- Demo and pilot are critical stages

### Marketing Channels
- Higher education conferences (APTA Campus Shuttles, CUPA, ACE)
- Industry publications (Campus Safety Magazine, University Business)
- Peer referrals (transportation directors have tight networks)
- Student government partnerships
- Campus sustainability networks

### Key Sales Activities

**1. RFP Response (Critical):**
- Monitor RFP databases and university procurement sites
- Respond to all RFPs from qualified universities
- Include case studies from peer institutions
- Address specific requirements (SSO, integrations, ADA compliance)

**2. Demo Strategy:**
- Show student-facing app first (student experience is #1)
- Demonstrate capacity management and safety features
- Walk through administrator dashboards (ridership reporting)
- Include student government in demo

**3. Pilot Program:**
- Start with one route (lowest risk)
- Run during low-volume period (summer or winter break)
- Gather student feedback actively
- Present pilot results with data

### Typical Objections & Responses

**"Our current system works fine."**
> "Student satisfaction data shows [X]% want real-time tracking. Your peer institutions have already upgraded. Can we show you what students expect?"

**"We can't afford to switch right now."**
> "Universities typically reduce operational costs by [X]% through better capacity management. Let's calculate your ROI."

**"Our IT department won't approve this."**
> "We integrate with campus SSO and meet all security requirements. Would a call with your IT team help?"

**"Students won't download another app."**
> "Universities see [X]% adoption in first semester with proper launch campaign. We provide communication templates and launch support."

## Success Profile

### Ideal University Customer

**Characteristics:**
- 10,000+ student population
- Multi-route system (5+ routes)
- Active student government (will champion new technology)
- Progressive transportation leadership
- Sustainability initiatives
- Budget for transportation innovation
- Plans to expand housing or campus

### Red Flags (Still Qualified, But Challenging)
- Very small budget (<$15K annually)
- No student input in decision process
- Outsourced all shuttle operations
- Recent major IT security breach
- Transportation director retiring soon

### Expansion Indicators

**Signs of Strong LTV:**
- Adding new routes annually
- Student ridership growing year-over-year
- Active program marketing to students
- Multi-year contracts
- Referrals to other universities
- Participation in case studies

**Upsell Opportunities:**
- Special event routing (game days, orientation)
- Advanced analytics for route optimization
- White-label app with university branding
- Integration with campus systems
- Late-night on-demand service (hybrid shuttle + micro-transit)

## Do's and Don'ts

### DO:
- Emphasize student experience and satisfaction first
- Reference peer institutions and competitors
- Address safety and Title IX considerations
- Provide sustainability metrics
- Offer pilot programs to reduce risk
- Include student government in the process
- Launch before fall semester
- Provide communication templates for student outreach

### DON'T:
- Lead with cost savings (student experience matters more)
- Ignore student feedback or government input
- Promise features requiring custom development
- Rush implementation during busy semester periods
- Neglect ADA compliance and accessibility
- Use corporate or operator language/case studies
- Assume fast sales cycles (be patient with committees)
